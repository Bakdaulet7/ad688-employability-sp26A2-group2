▸ Three views, one question

Where AI actually pays — and where it doesn't

The "AI premium" is real, but it's not evenly distributed. Some skills, career stages, and states see double-digit pay bumps; others see the opposite. Each chart below is interactive — hover for exact figures.

01
Software-skill analysis

Java leads the listed software skills, with SQL split sharply by label

Java postings show the highest average salary in this table at $137.2K, followed by Sql at $123.8K and .NET at $121.3K. Python sits near the middle of the group, while the uppercase SQL category is the lowest at $103.8K.

The gap suggests skill labels are picking up different role mixes, not just different tools. Backend and platform-oriented software skills cluster higher, while broader database-language postings appear more mixed in seniority and function.

02
Remote-work analysis

Remote roles edge out onsite roles by roughly $15.8K on average

Fully remote postings average $116.5K, slightly ahead of postings with no remote designation and hybrid remote roles. Not remote postings average $100.7K, making them the clear low point in this table.

The result lines up with the idea that remote-friendly roles are often more specialized or compete in broader labor markets, while onsite roles can reflect more local pay bands and a wider range of job families.

03
State salary analysis

Connecticut, New Jersey, and Arkansas top the salary ranking

The top three states are tightly packed: Connecticut averages $124.9K, New Jersey $122.9K, and Arkansas $122.2K. California appears sixth at $120.8K, close to Virginia, Vermont, Montana, and Illinois.

The spread across the top ten is relatively narrow, about $6.3K from Connecticut to Washington, D.C., suggesting the highest-paying geographies are clustered more tightly than the skill-level salary gaps.

01
Skill-level analysis

Generative AI commands a 49% premium — Excel pays 26% less

At the top of the salary ladder sit the skills that build AI systems — Generative AI (+49%), Kubernetes (+34%), Docker (+29%), and AWS (+27%). At the bottom sit the tools that consume their outputs — Excel (−26%), Power BI (−17%), and Tableau (−16%) all post salaries well below market median.

The takeaway: employers are paying steep premiums for AI infrastructure and ML engineering skills, while traditional analyst tooling has become commoditized. This is association, not causation — but the gap is striking and consistent across thousands of postings.

02
Experience-level analysis

The AI premium is largest for entry-level — and disappears at the top

For entry-level workers (0–2 years), AI postings pay 19.5% more than non-AI peers — a $15K bump on a median ~$80K base. The gap shrinks to roughly 10% for mid- and senior-level roles, then collapses to essentially zero at lead/principal (9+ years).

The pattern suggests AI is currently a ladder for early-career workers, not a multiplier at the top. Once you're senior enough, leadership and domain expertise dominate compensation, and the AI label no longer adds a separate premium.

03
Geographic analysis

Where the AI premium is biggest — not always where you'd guess

Among major tech states, Washington (+33%) and California (+20%) show the steepest AI premiums — consistent with cloud and AI hyperscalers concentrated there. Smaller-volume states like Rhode Island and South Carolina top the chart, but with thin samples (~30 AI postings each), treat as directional.

Kentucky inverts the pattern with a −17% premium: its AI postings skew toward lower-paying analyst work rather than ML engineering, a reminder that "AI jobs" isn't a monolithic category — composition matters as much as label.
